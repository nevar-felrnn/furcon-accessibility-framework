## Appendix B: Conditions & Accommodations Reference Index

*This appendix is a practical reference for accessibility staff. It covers conditions commonly encountered at conventions, how they may present in a convention environment, and what support approaches are helpful. It is not a clinical guide — staff are not expected to diagnose or assess. The goal is to help staff respond appropriately when an attendee discloses a condition or when staff observe someone who may need support.*

---

### A Note on Language

Language preferences vary by community and by individual. Some people prefer person-first language ("person with autism"); others prefer identity-first language ("autistic person"). When in doubt, follow the attendee's lead — use the language they use to describe themselves. Never use outdated or offensive terms, and avoid clinical framing in casual conversation.

The most important language principle: ask, don't assume. "How can I best support you?" respects the attendee's expertise about their own needs.

### Mobility & Physical Conditions

#### Mobility Impairments

**What it includes:** Conditions that affect a person's ability to walk, stand, or navigate spaces. This includes wheelchair and scooter users, people who use canes or walkers, people with prosthetic limbs, and people whose mobility fluctuates (good days and bad days are common).

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Difficulty navigating crowded spaces, narrow corridors, or areas without clear accessible routes
- Fatigue from longer-than-expected distances or terrain
- Needing to know elevator locations before committing to a route
- Arriving at the accessibility desk asking about equipment loans

**Helpful approaches:**
- Offer mobility aid loans proactively if the attendee appears to be struggling *(see Chapter 7)*
- Know accessible routes, elevator locations, and accessible bathroom locations — have maps available
- Offer to escort if the attendee would find that helpful; do not assume they want or need an escort
- If the attendee uses their own mobility aid, do not touch it without permission — it is an extension of their body

**What not to do:**
- Do not push a wheelchair or move a mobility aid without asking
- Do not tell someone they "don't look like they need" a wheelchair or aid — mobility needs are not always visible
- Do not block accessible routes or seating areas with equipment, signage, or people

---

#### Chronic Pain & Fatigue Conditions

**What it includes:** Conditions like fibromyalgia, ME/CFS (chronic fatigue syndrome), lupus, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, and many others that cause persistent pain, fatigue, or both. These are frequently invisible — the person may look fine while managing significant symptoms.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Needing to sit down more than expected, or needing seated options in spaces that don't have many
- Difficulty standing in lines for extended periods
- Fatigue that worsens over the course of a long day
- Requesting a badge emblem to signal ongoing support needs

**Helpful approaches:**
- Take the attendee's self-report seriously — they know their condition better than anyone
- Know where seating is available throughout the venue
- Offer quiet room access for rest
- Be understanding if an attendee needs to make multiple trips to the desk over the weekend — their needs may shift day to day

**What not to do:**
- Do not express skepticism about invisible conditions
- Do not suggest that pacing themselves or resting means they are not "really" enjoying the convention

### Deaf & Hard of Hearing

**What it includes:** A spectrum from mild hearing loss to profound deafness. Some people use hearing aids or cochlear implants; others do not. ASL (American Sign Language) is a distinct language with its own grammar and is not simply signed English. Not all deaf people use ASL.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Difficulty following verbal announcements or conversations in noisy spaces
- Needing to know where ASL interpretation is available
- Asking about captioning or CART services
- Preferring written communication

**Helpful approaches:**
- Face the person directly when speaking and do not cover your mouth
- Speak clearly and at a natural pace — do not exaggerate mouth movements
- If the person uses an interpreter, speak to the person, not the interpreter
- Know which programming spaces have ASL or CART coverage *(see Chapter 6)*
- Be prepared to write things down or use a phone screen if needed

**What not to do:**
- Do not shout — it does not help and can be uncomfortable for hearing aid users
- Do not assume a deaf attendee cannot communicate — they may communicate in multiple ways
- Do not speak to the interpreter instead of the attendee

### Blind & Low Vision

**What it includes:** A range of visual impairments from low vision (partial sight) to complete blindness. Many people with visual impairments have some functional vision. Guide dogs and white canes are common mobility tools.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Difficulty navigating unfamiliar spaces without landmarks or guidance
- Needing verbal descriptions of layouts, signage, or wayfinding
- Using a guide dog or white cane

**Helpful approaches:**
- Ask before offering assistance — "Would you like me to describe the layout?" or "Can I help guide you?"
- If guiding, offer your arm rather than grabbing theirs — let them take your elbow
- Describe your surroundings verbally if the attendee would find that helpful
- Never pet or distract a guide dog — it is working

**What not to do:**
- Do not grab or steer a person without asking
- Do not move a person's white cane
- Do not distract, feed, or engage with a guide dog without the handler's explicit permission
- Do not assume low vision means no vision — many people have partial sight

### Sensory Processing Differences

**What it includes:** Difficulty processing or regulating sensory input — sound, light, touch, smell, crowds. Common in autistic people and people with sensory processing disorder, but not limited to them. Can also be a feature of PTSD, anxiety, migraines, and other conditions.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Becoming overwhelmed in crowded, noisy, or brightly lit spaces
- Distress responses that may look like anxiety, agitation, or shutdown
- Seeking out quieter, lower-stimulation areas
- Hypersensitivity to specific sensory inputs (certain sounds, smells, textures)

**Helpful approaches:**
- Direct the attendee to the quiet room proactively *(see Chapter 11)*
- Offer sensory support items (earplugs, sunglasses, fidget tools) *(see Chapter 7)*
- Do not add stimulation to an already stimulating situation — speak calmly and at a lower volume
- Give the attendee space and time; do not rush the interaction

**What not to do:**
- Do not touch an attendee who is overwhelmed without asking — unexpected touch can worsen the situation
- Do not interpret shutdown (becoming non-verbal or very still) as rudeness or unresponsiveness — it is a stress response
- Do not push the attendee to "push through" sensory overload

### Autism Spectrum Conditions

**What it includes:** Autism is a neurological difference affecting social communication, sensory processing, and patterns of behavior and interest. It presents differently in every person. Many autistic people do not disclose or are not diagnosed.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Sensory overload (see above)
- Difficulty with unpredictable schedules or sudden changes
- Communication style differences — may be very direct, may prefer written communication, may have difficulty with implied meaning or social scripts
- May become distressed if routines are disrupted (e.g. a programming change, an unexpected crowd)

**Helpful approaches:**
- Be clear and direct — avoid vague, implied, or figurative language in important communications
- Give advance notice of changes when possible
- Take communication preferences seriously — if the attendee prefers to write rather than speak, accommodate that
- Sensory accommodations and quiet room access are often valuable

**What not to do:**
- Do not assume an autistic attendee cannot advocate for themselves
- Do not use functioning labels ("high-functioning," "low-functioning") — they are not useful and often harmful
- Do not interpret directness as rudeness

### Mental Health Conditions

#### Anxiety & Panic Disorders

**What it includes:** Conditions characterized by persistent worry, fear, or panic responses disproportionate to the immediate situation. Panic attacks are intense episodes of fear accompanied by physical symptoms (rapid heart rate, difficulty breathing, dizziness) that typically peak within 10 minutes.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Difficulty in large crowds or enclosed spaces
- Panic attacks — may appear as a person who is shaking, hyperventilating, or appearing to be in acute distress
- Avoidance of certain spaces or events due to anxiety
- Seeking reassurance or repeated check-ins with the desk

**Helpful approaches:**
- If an attendee is having a panic attack: stay calm, speak quietly and slowly, guide them to a quieter space, ask what helps them ("What usually helps you when this happens?")
- Do not tell someone having a panic attack to calm down or that there is nothing to worry about
- Quiet room access is often the right first step
- Panic attacks are frightening but not medically dangerous in themselves — they pass. If symptoms persist or worsen significantly, consult Safety

**What not to do:**
- Do not minimize or dismiss anxiety
- Do not crowd around a distressed attendee — one calm person is better than several concerned ones

---

#### PTSD & Trauma Responses

**What it includes:** Post-traumatic stress disorder results from exposure to traumatic events. Triggers — stimuli that activate a trauma response — vary widely and are not always predictable.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- Startle responses to sudden sounds or touches
- Avoidance of certain spaces, topics, or situations
- Acute distress responses that may seem disproportionate to the immediate context
- Dissociation — appearing detached or unresponsive

**Helpful approaches:**
- Respond to the attendee's current state, not to the logic of the trigger
- Quiet, calm, low-stimulation environments are typically helpful
- Ask before touching — unexpected physical contact can trigger a trauma response
- Follow the attendee's lead about what they need

**What not to do:**
- Do not ask about or probe the nature of the trauma
- Do not touch without asking
- Do not express disbelief or confusion about the response

### Diabetes & Metabolic Conditions

**What it includes:** Conditions affecting blood sugar regulation, including Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. Blood sugar episodes (hypoglycemia — low blood sugar; hyperglycemia — high blood sugar) can occur and require different responses.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- An attendee who becomes suddenly shaky, sweaty, confused, or irritable may be experiencing low blood sugar
- Needing to eat or drink at specific times regardless of programming schedules
- Needing to know where food and water are available throughout the venue *(see Chapter 4)*
- Carrying medical supplies (insulin, glucose tablets, a continuous glucose monitor)

**Helpful approaches:**
- If low blood sugar is suspected: offer fast-acting sugar (juice, glucose tablets, a sugary drink) if available; the attendee may carry their own supplies
- Know where food and water are available throughout the venue
- If the attendee is confused or unresponsive, involve Safety immediately

**What not to do:**
- Do not comment on what the attendee is eating or when — food timing is a medical necessity
- Do not assume an episode of confusion is intoxication

### Service Animals

**What it includes:** Trained animals (typically dogs) that perform specific tasks for a person with a disability. Under the ADA, service animals are permitted in all areas open to the public. Emotional support animals have different legal standing and are not covered by the same access provisions.

**How it may present at a convention:**
- An attendee accompanied by a dog in a vest or harness
- Questions from other attendees or staff about whether animals are permitted

**Helpful approaches:**
- Service animals are permitted in all convention spaces — do not ask the attendee to remove or contain the animal
- The only questions permitted under ADA guidelines are: "Is this a service animal required because of a disability?" and "What work or task has the dog been trained to perform?"
- Ensure other staff and convention personnel understand service animal access rights

**What not to do:**
- Do not pet, feed, or engage with the service animal without the handler's explicit permission — it is working
- Do not ask about the nature of the handler's disability
- Do not require documentation or certification — there is no official service animal registry

### Invisible Disabilities — General Guidance

Many attendees with disabilities will not appear visibly disabled. This is the norm, not the exception. Invisible disabilities include chronic pain, fatigue, autoimmune conditions, mental health conditions, neurological differences, and many others.

Key principles:
- **Believe the attendee.** If someone says they need support, take it at face value. It is not accessibility staff's role to evaluate or verify.
- **The badge emblem system exists precisely for this.** It allows attendees with invisible disabilities to register their needs without having to explain themselves repeatedly *(see Chapter 9)*.
- **Never comment on appearance.** "You don't look disabled" is not a compliment.
