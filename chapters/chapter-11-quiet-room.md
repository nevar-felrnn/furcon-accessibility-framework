## Chapter 11: Quiet Room

*The quiet room is a designated low-stimulation space where attendees can decompress, manage sensory overload, or simply take a break from the convention environment. It is one of the most valued accessibility offerings a convention can provide, and one of the least resource-intensive to establish. Done well, it is a place attendees return to year after year and tell others about.*

---

### Purpose

The quiet room serves attendees who need a temporary reprieve from the noise, crowds, lights, and social demands of the convention. This includes — but is not limited to — attendees with:
- Sensory processing sensitivities or autism spectrum conditions
- Anxiety, PTSD, or other mental health conditions that can be exacerbated by overstimulation
- Migraines or other conditions triggered by noise or light
- General fatigue or the need for a non-social rest space

The quiet room is a space for recovery, not a medical treatment room. It is not a substitute for emergency mental health intervention. If an attendee in the quiet room requires more support than rest and calm can provide, follow the escalation procedures outlined below.

---

### Location

The quiet room location should be confirmed during the venue assessment *(see Chapter 4: Venue Assessment)* and coordinated with IT and convention operations well before the event. A good quiet room location is:

- **Low-traffic** — away from main programming areas, hallways, and anywhere with significant ambient noise
- **Accessible** — reachable without stairs; accessible for mobility aid users
- **Easy to find** — can be signed from a main attendee area without requiring a long or confusing walk
- **Stable** — the room should not change during the convention or be repurposed for other uses

A hotel room or small meeting room works well. A repurposed closet or storage room does not.

---

### Physical Setup

The quiet room should be set up to minimize stimulation and maximize comfort:

**Lighting:**
- Dim or adjustable lighting is strongly preferred — harsh overhead fluorescent lighting undermines the room's purpose
- If the room has windows, ensure blackout curtains or blinds are available
- A simple lamp or string lights as an alternative to overhead fixtures can make a significant difference

**Seating & Rest:**
- Comfortable seating is essential — chairs, floor cushions, a couch, or a combination
- A mat or blanket for attendees who need to lie down is a valuable addition
- Avoid hard chairs or seating that is uncomfortable for extended rest

**Sensory Environment:**
- Keep the room quiet — confirm with the venue that noise from adjacent spaces will not be an issue
- Avoid scented items (air fresheners, candles) which can be a sensory trigger for some attendees
- Consider soft background white noise or the option for silence — confirm what works best based on room acoustics

**Supplies:**
- Water available for attendees
- A small selection of sensory support items (fidget tools, stress balls) kept in an unobtrusive location
- Tissues
- Clear, simple signage at the entrance explaining the room's purpose and guidelines

---

### Room Guidelines

Post simple guidelines at the entrance and inside the room. Keep them brief and non-clinical:

- This is a quiet space — please keep noise to a minimum, including phone calls
- Phones on silent
- The room is available to any attendee who needs it — no sign-in required for entry
- Please be respectful of others using the space
- If you need help, accessibility staff are available at [desk location]

The quiet room is not a sleeping room, though attendees who doze while resting should not be disturbed unless there is a welfare concern.

---

### Hours

The quiet room should be open during all core convention hours. If programming extends significantly into the evening, consider whether extended quiet room hours are warranted. Confirm hours during pre-con planning and publish them in convention-facing materials.

If the quiet room cannot be staffed or camera-monitored outside core hours, close it rather than leave it unsupervised and unsupported.

---

### Monitoring & IT Coordination

The quiet room should be monitored by camera, coordinated with the IT department. Camera monitoring serves two purposes:
- Welfare checks — staff can check in on the room without disturbing attendees
- Incident response — if a situation escalates, there is a record and a way to observe without entering

Coordinate with IT during pre-con planning to confirm:
- Camera placement that covers the room without being intrusive
- Who has access to the camera feed and how to access it
- The process for requesting a welfare check through IT

Staff should not enter the quiet room unnecessarily. A camera welfare check is preferable to a physical intrusion whenever the situation permits.

---

### Staff Protocol

Accessibility staff are responsible for awareness of the quiet room throughout the convention, but should not station themselves inside it. The room's value depends on it feeling private and unmonitored.

**Routine monitoring:**
- Periodically check the camera feed to confirm the room is being used appropriately and that no attendee appears to be in distress
- Note the room in daily briefings — flag any patterns (consistently overcrowded, reported issues, etc.)

**If an attendee appears to be in distress:**
1. Assess via camera feed first if possible — determine whether the attendee appears to need intervention or is simply resting
2. If intervention appears warranted, a single staff member should enter calmly and quietly
3. Approach gently: "Hi, I just wanted to check in — are you doing okay? Is there anything I can get you?"
4. If the attendee indicates they are fine or just resting, respect that and leave
5. If the attendee needs support beyond rest, follow the escalation path to the Director and, if appropriate, Safety *(see Chapter 12: Cross-Department Coordination)*
6. If the situation involves a medical emergency, contact Safety immediately

**What staff should not do:**
- Do not send multiple people into the quiet room at once
- Do not ask attendees to leave unless the convention is closing or there is a safety issue
- Do not discuss what you observed in the quiet room with people outside the accessibility team

---

### Communication to Attendees

The quiet room should be prominently mentioned in convention-facing accessibility information:
- Listed on the convention website and app
- Included in the convention program or guide
- Mentioned in accessibility desk signage
- Staff should proactively mention it to attendees who appear to be struggling

Many attendees who would benefit from the quiet room do not know it exists or do not think they are "allowed" to use it. Make it easy to find and easy to feel welcome in.
