## Chapter 4: Venue Assessment

*The venue assessment is one of the most critical pre-con activities for the accessibility department. A thorough assessment identifies barriers before they become problems and informs decisions about desk placement, equipment needs, staffing, and coordination with other departments. The assessment should be conducted as early as possible — ideally when the venue is confirmed — and revisited if the venue layout changes.*

---

### Who Conducts the Assessment

The assessment is led by the Accessibility Director or Assistant Director. Where possible, additional staff should participate — more perspectives mean more potential issues identified. Coordinate with the venue contact to arrange access before the convention.

---

### What to Assess

#### Pathways & Passages
- Identify narrow corridors, tight corners, or confined spaces that may be difficult for mobility aid users or attendees with claustrophobia or sensory sensitivities
- Note any passages that become high-traffic chokepoints during peak hours
- Identify all entrances and exits and their accessibility (automatic doors, ramps, width)

#### Vertical Movement
- Location of all elevators, escalators, and staircases
- Elevator capacity and proximity to high-traffic areas
- Identify any situations where primary event flow uses stairs without a nearby accessible alternative (e.g. fursuit parades, programming transitions between floors)
- Flag any conflicts to the relevant department for route modification *(see Chapter 12: Cross-Department Coordination)*

#### Programming & Event Spaces
- Assess each programming space for accessible seating placement opportunities
- Note sight lines, acoustics, and lighting conditions
- Identify spaces where hearing or vision accommodations may be particularly needed
- Assess dealer's den, artist alley, and any other attendee-facing spaces for spacing and mobility aid navigation

#### Bathrooms
- Location of all ADA/handicap capable bathrooms throughout the venue
- Note proximity to major programming and attendee spaces
- Flag any gaps in coverage (areas of the venue far from accessible facilities)

#### Registration & Accessibility Desk
- Assess registration area for accessibility — door width, space, congestion potential
- Confirm accessibility desk location offers adequate space, ceiling height, and no access barriers
- Confirm desk is visible and easy for attendees to locate

#### Acoustics
- General acoustic quality in each space
- Identify spaces where hearing may be particularly difficult
- Note where ASL interpreter placement would be most effective

#### Food & Amenity Proximity
- Location of food vendors, concessions, or nearby restaurants relative to programming spaces
- Note any significant distances that may be challenging for attendees with conditions affected by food timing or access (e.g. diabetes)
- Identify water access points throughout the venue

#### Quiet Room
- Confirm location is accessible, low-traffic, and easy to find
- Confirm IT camera placement is feasible *(see Chapter 11: Quiet Room)*

---

### Documentation

All findings should be recorded in the convention-specific venue assessment document. Where possible, photograph or sketch problem areas. Findings should be shared with relevant departments promptly so adjustments can be made before schedules and layouts are finalized.

### A Note on Event Planning Coordination

Accessibility should be an active participant in event planning conversations, not just a reviewer of finished plans. Many accessibility conflicts — such as parade routes that bypass elevator access — can be resolved easily when caught early but become difficult to address once publicized. Accessibility should request to review major event layouts and schedules before they are finalized. *(see Chapter 12: Cross-Department Coordination)*
