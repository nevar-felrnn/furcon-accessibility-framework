## Chapter 13: At-Con Operations

*All the planning, recruiting, and coordinating comes down to what happens at the convention itself. At-con operations are the execution phase — running the desk, supporting attendees, managing incidents, and keeping the team aligned across a long and often intense weekend. This chapter covers the rhythm of convention operations and how to handle the situations that come up.*

---

### Before the Convention Opens

**Setup:**
Arrive early enough to have the desk fully set up before attendees begin arriving. The desk should be ready before badge pickup opens — attendees with accessibility needs may arrive at the very start of registration.

Setup checklist:
- Desk stocked with all supplies *(see Chapter 10: Accessibility Desk Operations)*
- Accessibility log initialized
- Badge emblems ready and supply confirmed
- At-con contact sheet distributed to all staff *(see Chapter 12)*
- Quiet room confirmed open and camera confirmed live with IT
- Staff assignments and schedule confirmed

**Pre-con briefing:**
Hold a brief team meeting before doors open. Cover:
- Schedule for the day — desk shifts, floor coverage, briefing times
- Any last-minute changes to staffing, venue, or procedures
- Reminders on key protocols: intake, escalation, confidentiality
- Open floor for questions

Keep the briefing short and energizing. Staff are about to work a long day.

---

### Daily Rhythm

A typical convention day for the accessibility department:

**Start of day:**
- Daily briefing with full team (10–15 minutes) *(see Chapter 8)*
- Desk opens, floor coverage begins
- Quiet room confirmed open

**During the day:**
- Desk staffed continuously during operating hours
- Floor coverage staff circulating through attendee spaces
- Periodic camera welfare checks on the quiet room
- Director or Assistant Director available for escalations
- Ongoing logging of all attendee interactions

**End of day:**
- Shift handoffs completed with full briefing *(see Chapter 10)*
- Equipment on loan accounted for — follow up on anything not returned
- Accessibility log secured
- Quiet room closed and confirmed clear
- Director reviews the day's log and flags any open items for tomorrow

---

### Attendee Interactions

Most at-con interactions fall into a small number of categories. Staff should be prepared for all of them.

**Accommodation requests:**
An attendee requests a specific accommodation — accessible seating, advance entry to an event, a mobility aid loan, a badge emblem, or something else. Listen fully before responding. Provide what the department can provide; be honest about what it cannot. Document the interaction. If the request requires coordination with another department, facilitate that connection rather than leaving the attendee to navigate it alone.

**Information requests:**
An attendee asks where the quiet room is, where accessible bathrooms are, whether ASL interpretation is available, or other factual questions. Answer clearly and offer to walk the attendee there if needed. Update signage or desk materials if the same question is asked repeatedly — it may indicate a gap in the convention's public-facing information.

**Equipment loans:**
Follow the equipment loan procedure *(see Chapter 10)*. Demonstrate safe use if needed. Log the loan. Follow up if equipment is not returned at the expected time.

**Distressed attendees:**
An attendee arrives at the desk or is encountered on the floor in an emotional, anxious, or overwhelmed state. Respond calmly and without urgency — matching the attendee's energy rarely helps. Give the attendee space to be heard before offering solutions. The quiet room is often the right first step. Do not push the attendee toward a resolution faster than they are ready for. If the attendee's distress exceeds what rest and calm can address, follow the escalation path below.

**Complaints:**
An attendee raises a complaint — about an accessibility gap, about another attendee's behavior, about the convention's handling of a situation. Listen fully. Do not be defensive. Document the complaint. If it is within the department's power to address, do so. If it involves another department or convention leadership, route it appropriately and let the attendee know what will happen next. Follow up.

---

### Escalation

Not every situation can or should be handled at the desk. Know when to escalate and to whom.

**Escalate to the Director or Assistant Director when:**
- An attendee situation is beyond the training or authority of the staff member handling it
- A complaint involves a serious allegation or requires a formal response
- A situation is likely to involve media, legal, or leadership attention
- A staff member needs support or relief

**Escalate to Safety when:**
- There is a risk to the physical safety of the attendee or others
- A medical emergency is occurring or suspected
- Physical intervention may be required
- The situation involves a weapon, controlled substance, or criminal activity

**Escalation is not failure.** Staff who escalate appropriately are doing their jobs correctly. A staff member who handles a situation beyond their authority — or who avoids escalating because they are unsure — creates larger problems. Make escalation easy and expected.

When escalating, provide the receiving party with:
- A brief summary of the situation
- The attendee's location
- What has already been done or said
- What you need from them

---

### Confidentiality

Attendee information gathered through accessibility operations — intake forms, log entries, conversations — is confidential. It is not discussed with other attendees, shared with other departments without the attendee's consent, or posted in any public or semi-public channel.

Staff talking about a situation among themselves is acceptable for coordination purposes. Gossiping about an attendee is not. The distinction matters.

If another department requests information about an attendee, the default answer is to ask the attendee's consent before sharing anything beyond what is necessary to support them. In an emergency involving physical safety, share what Safety needs to respond — nothing more.

The accessibility log should never be left unattended in a visible location. At the end of each day, secure it.

### Tracking Open Items

Throughout the convention, situations will arise that cannot be fully resolved in the moment — a follow-up needed with Registration, an equipment return outstanding, a complaint being reviewed by leadership. Track these explicitly.

Designate a section of the accessibility log (or a separate tracking method) for open items. Review open items at the start of each daily briefing. Do not let items fall through the cracks because the convention is busy.

---

### Staff Welfare

The accessibility department asks a great deal of its people. Over a long convention weekend, staff can experience compassion fatigue, emotional exhaustion, or burnout — particularly after difficult interactions with distressed attendees.

The Director should actively watch for signs of strain in staff:
- Shortened patience or uncharacteristically curt interactions
- Withdrawal from team communication
- Reluctance to take on additional interactions

Concrete steps:
- Ensure staff take real breaks away from the desk — not just slow periods at the desk
- Check in with staff individually, not just at group briefings
- Give staff permission to say "I need a break" without it reflecting poorly on them
- After a particularly difficult interaction, check in with the staff member who handled it

The department cannot support attendees well if its own team is running on empty.

---

### Convention Close

At the end of the final day of the convention:

- All equipment confirmed returned and inventoried
- Accessibility log secured and retained for post-con review
- Quiet room cleared, closed, and confirmed with IT (camera deactivated if applicable)
- Desk broken down and supplies accounted for
- Director thanks staff in person before they leave
- Post-con debrief scheduled *(see Chapter 14)*

Do not let staff simply drift away at close without acknowledgment. The post-con debrief is important, but a personal thank-you at the end of the weekend is immediate and meaningful.
