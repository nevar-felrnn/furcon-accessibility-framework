## Chapter 8: Staff Recruitment & Training

*Accessibility staff are the department in practice. The quality of the attendee experience depends almost entirely on the people doing this work — their warmth, their judgment, and their preparation. Recruitment and training are not administrative tasks; they are the most important things a director does.*

---

### Recruitment

#### When to Start

Staff recruitment should begin no later than 3 months before the convention, with a target headcount confirmed by the 30-day mark. Starting earlier is better — accessibility staff are drawn from the same volunteer pool as the rest of the convention, and popular roles fill quickly.

#### Who to Recruit

Accessibility staff do not need formal credentials or prior experience in disability services. What matters most:

- **Warmth and approachability** — staff will be the first point of contact for attendees in vulnerable situations
- **Good judgment** — knowing when to act, when to ask, and when to escalate
- **Discretion** — attendees share personal health and disability information; confidentiality must be treated seriously
- **Patience** — some interactions take time and cannot be rushed
- **Physical stamina** — accessibility work involves extended periods on foot and occasional physical assistance

Physical capability requirements vary by role. Staff who will assist with mobility aid transport should be comfortable doing so. Not all roles require this, and the department benefits from a range of people.

Prior experience as a convention volunteer is helpful but not required. Some of the best accessibility staff come from outside the typical volunteer pipeline — nurses, social workers, teachers, and people with personal or family experience of disability often bring directly applicable skills.

#### Where to Find Staff

- The convention's existing volunteer sign-up system
- Direct outreach to returning volunteers from previous years
- Word of mouth within the convention community
- Outreach to people in care-oriented professions or with relevant lived experience

The Accessibility Director should not rely solely on passive sign-up channels. Direct personal recruitment is more effective for a role that requires specific qualities.

#### Staffing Targets

Minimum staffing targets depend on the convention's size and operating hours. As a starting point:

- The accessibility desk should never be unstaffed during core convention hours
- At least two people should be present at the desk at peak periods
- Additional float staff for proactive floor coverage are valuable but secondary to desk coverage

Establish your target headcount based on desk hours and floor coverage goals, then recruit to exceed that number slightly to account for no-shows and schedule conflicts.

### Training

#### Timing

Training should be completed before the convention opens. Distribute written training materials at least two weeks in advance. A live training session — in person or remote — should be held in the 30 days before the event, after staff are confirmed but with enough lead time for questions and follow-up.

New staff require full training. Returning staff should receive a refresher covering any changes to procedures, equipment, or the venue.

#### Core Training Topics

**Department Mission & Approach**
- The department's mission and scope *(see Chapter 1)*
- The proactive approach: identifying attendees who may benefit from support and offering assistance without waiting to be asked
- Language and framing: how to approach attendees warmly, without assumption, and in a way that preserves dignity

**Disability Awareness**
- Overview of the types of disabilities the department commonly serves: mobility, sensory, hearing, vision, cognitive, and invisible disabilities
- The importance of asking how to help rather than assuming
- Understanding that many disabilities are not visible
- What not to say — common well-intentioned phrases that can be harmful

**Roles & Responsibilities**
- Each staff member's specific role and shift responsibilities *(see Chapter 2)*
- How to escalate issues to staff, Assistant Director, or Director
- Who is responsible for what during different scenarios

**Intake & Documentation**
- How to conduct an attendee intake interaction: greeting, listening, documenting needs and outcomes
- What information to record and how
- Confidentiality expectations — attendee information is not shared outside the department without consent *(see Chapter 13: At-Con Operations)*

**Equipment Operation**
- Safe and correct use of each type of mobility aid the convention provides *(see Chapter 7)*
- Equipment storage and retrieval procedures
- What to do if equipment is damaged or unavailable

**Badge Emblem System**
- How the badge emblem system works and what it indicates
- Staff role in identifying and responding to emblem holders *(see Chapter 9)*

**Quiet Room**
- Location, hours, and purpose of the quiet room
- What staff should and should not do if an attendee is in distress in the quiet room
- When to involve IT and when to involve Safety *(see Chapter 11)*

**Cross-Department Contacts**
- Key contacts at Safety, IT, and Registration and when to reach out to each
- How to communicate with other departments during the convention *(see Chapter 12)*

**Escalation Procedures**
- When to escalate to the Director or Assistant Director
- How to handle a situation that exceeds your role or training — it is always acceptable to say "let me get someone who can help better"

#### Training Format

A combination of written materials and a live session is more effective than either alone. Suggested format:

1. **Written training packet** distributed in advance — staff can review at their own pace and come to the live session with questions
2. **Live training session** covering key topics, scenario walkthroughs, and Q&A — 60–90 minutes is typically sufficient
3. **Pre-con briefing** at the start of the convention to review the schedule, confirm assignments, and address any last-minute changes

Scenarios and role-play are particularly useful for building staff confidence in interactions that may feel awkward or uncertain — approaching an attendee proactively, handling a disclosure of disability, or managing a distressed attendee.

#### At-Con Daily Briefings

Brief daily check-ins at the start of each shift keep staff aligned and surface issues early. These do not need to be long — 10 to 15 minutes is sufficient. Cover:

- Any notable interactions or unresolved issues from the previous day
- Schedule or assignment changes
- Reminders or updates relevant to the day's programming
- Open floor for staff questions or concerns

### Retention & Recognition

Accessibility work is emotionally demanding. Staff should feel seen, supported, and appreciated. Concrete steps:

- Thank staff personally and specifically — generic appreciation is less meaningful than acknowledging specific contributions
- Ensure staff take breaks and do not burn out over the course of the weekend
- Advocate for staff recognition within the convention's broader rewards program *(see Chapter 5: Budget & Resources)*
- Conduct a post-con debrief with the team before they leave the convention — this closes the experience intentionally and gives staff a chance to be heard *(see Chapter 14: Post-Con & Continuous Improvement)*

Staff who feel valued return. Retention matters because trained, experienced staff are substantially more effective than new recruits.
