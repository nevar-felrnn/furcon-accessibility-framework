## Chapter 10: Accessibility Desk Operations

*The accessibility desk is the department's home base and the primary point of contact for attendees seeking support. How the desk is set up, staffed, and operated directly shapes every attendee's first impression of the department. A well-run desk is welcoming, visible, and calm — even when it's busy.*

---

### Location

The desk location should be confirmed during the venue assessment *(see Chapter 4: Venue Assessment)* and coordinated with convention operations well before the event. A good desk location is:

- **Visible and easy to find** — near registration or a main attendee thoroughfare, not tucked in a corner
- **Accessible** — no stairs, no narrow approaches, enough space for a mobility aid user to approach comfortably
- **Quiet enough to have a conversation** — avoid placing the desk directly adjacent to high-noise areas like the main programming room entrance or dealer's den during peak hours
- **Stable** — the desk should not need to move during the convention

If the ideal location is not available, prioritize accessibility and visibility over convenience for staff.

---

### Hours

The desk should be staffed during all core convention hours. Confirm desk hours during pre-con planning and communicate them clearly to attendees through the convention's public-facing channels.

At minimum:
- Open when badge pickup / registration opens on the first day
- Open during all main programming hours
- Staffed continuously — no unattended gaps during operating hours

After-hours coverage is not required at the desk, but the Director or a designated on-call staff member should be reachable for urgent matters outside desk hours.

---

### Physical Setup

Before the convention opens, ensure the desk is stocked and ready:

**Required:**
- Accessibility log / intake forms (paper or digital — see Attendee Intake below)
- Badge emblems and application supplies *(see Chapter 9: Badge Emblem System)*
- Convention map with accessible routes, elevator locations, quiet room, and accessible bathrooms marked
- Contact list for key cross-department contacts (Safety, IT, Registration) *(see Chapter 12)*
- Staff schedule and shift assignments

**Recommended:**
- Sensory support items available for attendees (fidget toys, earplugs, etc.) *(see Chapter 7)*
- Water for staff
- A small supply of common comfort items (band-aids, pain relievers if convention policy permits)
- Printed copies of relevant policies (accommodation request process, quiet room guidelines)

Keep the desk surface clear and organized. A cluttered desk signals chaos; a tidy desk signals competence.

---

### Attendee Intake

When an attendee comes to the desk for support, the intake interaction should be warm, efficient, and confidential. The basic flow:

1. **Greet** the attendee and make them feel welcome — do not make them wait while staff finish other tasks
2. **Listen** — let the attendee explain their needs before offering solutions
3. **Ask** clarifying questions as needed — "How can I best support you this weekend?" is a good opener
4. **Document** the interaction: attendee name or badge number, needs identified, accommodations provided or arranged, and any follow-up required
5. **Apply badge emblem** if appropriate *(see Chapter 9)*
6. **Explain** what support is available and how to access it
7. **Invite** the attendee to return if their needs change or they need further assistance

Keep intake conversations private. If the desk is in a high-traffic area and a conversation is sensitive, step to a quieter spot or lower your voice. Never discuss an attendee's needs within earshot of other attendees.

---

### Accessibility Log

All attendee interactions should be logged. The log serves multiple purposes:
- Tracks accommodations in progress (e.g. equipment on loan, follow-ups needed)
- Provides data for post-con review and future planning
- Creates a record in case of disputes or incidents

At minimum, each log entry should capture:
- Date and time
- Attendee identifier (name or badge number — do not log more personal information than necessary)
- Nature of the request or need
- What was provided or arranged
- Any follow-up required and by whom

The log is confidential. It should not be left unattended in a visible or accessible location, and access should be limited to accessibility staff.

---

### Equipment Loans

When loaning mobility aids or other equipment to attendees:

- Record the loan in the accessibility log (item, attendee identifier, time out)
- Confirm the attendee knows how to use the equipment safely, or demonstrate if needed
- Note the expected return time if applicable
- Record when equipment is returned and inspect it before returning it to storage

If equipment is not returned, follow up with the attendee directly rather than waiting. Most non-returns are unintentional.

---

### Shift Handoffs

At the end of each shift, the outgoing staff member should brief the incoming staff member on:
- Any open intake interactions or follow-ups in progress
- Equipment currently on loan
- Any notable attendee situations to be aware of
- Desk supply levels (emblems, forms, etc.)

Do not leave the desk until the incoming staff member has arrived and been briefed. Gaps in coverage, even brief ones, are when problems occur.

---

### Managing a Busy Desk

During peak periods — registration opening, major programming transitions — the desk may receive more attendees than usual. Strategies for managing volume:

- Prioritize urgent needs (an attendee in distress) over routine requests
- Keep non-urgent intake efficient — gather the necessary information and move forward
- Ask waiting attendees to take a seat if available; acknowledge them so they know they have been seen
- Call in additional staff or volunteers if volume exceeds capacity

A long wait at the accessibility desk is a failure of staffing, not of the individual staff member. Flag staffing shortfalls during daily briefings.

---

### Proactive Floor Coverage

The desk is the department's home base, but the department's reach extends beyond it. Staff and volunteers on floor coverage should:

- Circulate through high-traffic attendee areas during busy periods
- Watch for attendees who appear to be struggling — navigating with a mobility aid, appearing overwhelmed or distressed, visibly confused about the venue
- Approach proactively and offer assistance warmly: "Hi, I'm with accessibility — can I help you find something or get you anything?"
- Direct attendees to the desk for intake if they have ongoing needs

Floor coverage is not surveillance. The goal is to make the department's presence felt throughout the venue, not just at the desk.
