## Chapter 14: Post-Con & Continuous Improvement

*The convention ends, but the department's work does not. What happens after the convention determines whether the next one is better. A team that debrefs honestly, documents what it learned, and follows through on its recommendations will improve year over year. A team that disperses without review will repeat the same gaps indefinitely.*

---

### Why Post-Con Review Matters

Post-con review serves three purposes:

1. **Capture knowledge before it fades.** Memory of specific incidents, gaps, and wins degrades quickly after a busy weekend. The window for accurate recall is short.

2. **Give staff a chance to be heard.** The team spent a demanding weekend in service of others. Closing that experience intentionally — with space for reflection and acknowledgment — matters for staff retention and wellbeing.

3. **Drive actual improvement.** Review without follow-through is theater. The value is in the recommendations that make it into next year's planning.

---

### Timing

The post-con debrief should happen as soon as practical after the convention — ideally within one to two weeks, while details are still fresh. Waiting longer risks losing the specificity that makes review useful.

A short informal debrief at the end of the final convention day *(see Chapter 13)* is valuable for immediate reactions, but does not replace a proper post-con review. The two serve different purposes.

---

### Who Participates

At minimum: the Director and Assistant Director.

Ideally: all staff who worked the convention, plus any key cross-department contacts who have relevant perspective (e.g. Safety, Registration).

Volunteers may be included at the Director's discretion, particularly if they handled notable situations or have useful feedback.

The review can be conducted in person, remotely, or through a written survey if scheduling a live session proves difficult. A live session is preferable — written surveys capture what people think to write down; conversations surface what people did not know they needed to say.

---

### What to Cover

Structure the review around four questions:

**What worked well?**
Identify what went right and why. These are practices worth repeating and, where applicable, formalizing. Record them explicitly — it is easy to lose good practices when leadership changes.

**What did not work or fell short?**
Identify gaps, failures, and near-misses. Be specific: not "communication was hard" but "we did not have a working radio channel to Safety until day two." Vague feedback produces vague improvements.

**What did we not have that we needed?**
Identify unmet attendee needs and department resource gaps. These are the inputs to next year's budget request and planning priorities.

**What should we do differently?**
Translate observations into specific, actionable recommendations. Each recommendation should have an owner and a target date where possible.

---

### Reviewing the Accessibility Log

Before the debrief, the Director should review the full accessibility log from the convention. Look for:

- Patterns in attendee requests or needs — what came up repeatedly?
- Unmet needs or situations that were not handled as well as they could have been
- Equipment or resource gaps (ran out of something, needed something that wasn't there)
- Interactions that required escalation and whether the escalation path worked
- Any open items from the at-con log that need resolution

Bring specific observations from the log into the debrief. Concrete examples make for better discussion than generalities.

---

### Documentation

The output of the post-con review should be a written summary documenting:

- Key observations (what worked, what didn't, what was missing)
- Specific recommendations, each with a named owner and target date
- Any decisions made (e.g. "we will not attempt badge emblem application at registration next year until the system is updated")
- Acknowledgment of staff contributions worth noting

Store this document somewhere the next planning cycle can find it. It should be referenced explicitly at the start of the next convention's planning — the "Before the Planning Cycle Begins" milestone in Chapter 3.

---

### Updating the Framework

If the post-con review surfaces practices, procedures, or knowledge that should be captured in this framework — update it. The framework is a living document precisely because operational experience is the best source of improvement.

Changes worth making to the framework:
- A process that worked and should be standardized
- A gap that is likely to recur at other conventions
- A new scenario or attendee need that the framework does not currently address
- A section that turned out to be unclear or incomplete in practice

Changes that belong in the convention-specific configuration, not the framework:
- Details specific to one venue or one convention's staff
- Decisions that reflect one convention's constraints rather than general best practice

When in doubt, lean toward updating the framework — shared knowledge benefits everyone.

### Recommendations to Leadership

Submit a written summary of post-con recommendations to convention leadership promptly after the review. The summary should be concise and action-oriented:

- What the department needs for next year (budget, staffing, resources)
- What coordination or process changes are needed from other departments
- What worked well and should be continued or expanded

Submit this while leadership is still in post-con mode and receptive to feedback. Recommendations submitted months later, without the context of fresh memory, carry less weight.

---

### Staff Recognition

Post-con is the time to formally recognize staff contributions. This should happen in two forms:

**Personal acknowledgment:**
The Director should personally thank each staff member — not a group email, but a direct, specific message acknowledging what that person contributed. This takes time and is worth it.

**Formal recognition:**
Ensure staff are recognized through the convention's formal recognition channels — whatever the convention uses for staff acknowledgment (shirts, awards, social media shoutouts, etc.). Advocate for accessibility staff to be included.

Recognition is not a nice-to-have. It is a retention tool. Staff who feel seen return. Staff who feel invisible do not.

---

### Beginning the Next Cycle

The final step of post-con is the first step of next year. Before the team fully disperses:

- Confirm whether the Director and Assistant Director are returning for the next convention
- If not, begin succession planning immediately — institutional knowledge is most transferable right after a convention, not six months later
- Note any time-sensitive items that need action before the next planning cycle formally begins (e.g. renewing interpreter vendor relationships, storing equipment properly)

The planning timeline in Chapter 3 starts well before most people think about it. Post-con is the right time to confirm who will be at the helm when it does.
