## Chapter 9: Badge Emblem System

*A badge emblem is a small, discreet marker added to an attendee's badge that signals to trained staff that the attendee has registered accessibility needs. It allows attendees to communicate their needs once, at registration or the accessibility desk, rather than repeatedly explaining themselves throughout the weekend. It also allows staff to proactively offer appropriate assistance without the attendee having to ask.*

---

### Purpose

The badge emblem system serves two functions:

1. **For attendees** — it reduces the burden of repeatedly disclosing a disability or need. An attendee who has already completed intake does not have to re-explain their situation every time they interact with a staff member or volunteer.

2. **For staff** — it provides a quiet signal that an attendee may benefit from a check-in or additional support, without requiring the attendee to initiate.

The emblem does not indicate what an attendee's specific needs are. It is a signal to pay attention and be ready to assist, not a summary of the attendee's disability or accommodation.

---

### What the Emblem Is

The specific form of the emblem — a sticker, a printed element on the badge, a colored dot, a ribbon — is determined by each convention and documented in the convention-specific configuration. The emblem should be:

- **Discreet** — visible to someone looking at the badge, but not immediately obvious to casual passersby
- **Non-stigmatizing** — should not look like a warning label or mark the attendee as different in a negative way
- **Consistent** — all emblems for a given convention should look identical so staff can recognize them reliably

Avoid designs that are so subtle that staff miss them, or so prominent that the attendee feels marked.

---

### How Attendees Get the Emblem

Emblems may be applied through one of two paths:

#### At Registration
For attendees who have pre-registered accessibility needs, the emblem can be applied during badge pickup. This requires prior coordination with the Registration department to ensure the flag is visible in the registration system and that registration staff know how to apply the emblem correctly. *(see Chapter 12: Cross-Department Coordination)*

This path is convenient for attendees who know their needs in advance and reduces the need for a separate stop at the accessibility desk.

#### At the Accessibility Desk
Attendees who did not pre-register, or who realize they need support after arriving, can come to the accessibility desk to complete intake and receive their emblem. This is the fallback path and will apply to many attendees.

Both paths may be in use simultaneously. Confirm with Registration which path they are prepared to support.

---

### What the Emblem Does and Does Not Mean

**It means:**
- The attendee has registered with the accessibility department
- Staff should be attentive and ready to assist if the attendee appears to need support
- A proactive check-in ("Is there anything I can help you with?") is appropriate

**It does not mean:**
- The attendee needs constant assistance or monitoring
- Staff know what the attendee's specific needs or accommodations are
- The attendee cannot or will not ask for help on their own

The emblem is an invitation to be attentive — not a directive to hover. Many attendees with an emblem will go about their weekend without needing any additional interaction. Others will appreciate a warm check-in. Use judgment.

---

### Privacy

The emblem system handles sensitive personal information. Staff should treat all knowledge of an attendee's emblem, intake information, or accommodations as confidential. Specific needs are documented in the accessibility log and are not discussed outside the department without the attendee's consent. *(see Chapter 13: At-Con Operations)*

Registration staff who apply emblems as part of the badge pickup process should be briefed that the emblem is an accessibility marker and that attendee information is confidential. They do not need to know more than that.

---

### Staff Training on the Emblem System

All accessibility staff and volunteers should be trained to:
- Recognize the emblem on a badge
- Understand what it signals and what it does not
- Respond appropriately — a natural, warm offer of assistance rather than a formal or clinical interaction
- Not draw attention to the emblem in front of other attendees

Training scenarios involving emblem interactions are useful for building staff confidence. *(see Chapter 8: Staff Recruitment & Training)*

---

### Cost & Logistics

The cost of producing emblems — whether stickers, printed badge elements, or another form — should be addressed in the budget process. Confirm with Registration whether this is a shared cost or falls entirely under accessibility. *(see Chapter 5: Budget & Resources)*

Ensure a sufficient supply of emblems is available at the accessibility desk throughout the convention for walk-in intake. Running out of emblems mid-convention undermines the system.
