## Chapter 12: Cross-Department Coordination

*Accessibility does not operate in isolation. Many of the department's most important functions depend on relationships with other parts of the convention — Safety, IT, Registration, and Programming each play a role in making the convention work for attendees with disabilities. These relationships need to be built before the convention, not improvised during it.*

---

### General Principles

**Start early.** Cross-department conversations should begin months before the convention, not weeks. Decisions about badge systems, event layouts, and quiet room placement are much easier to influence before they are finalized.

**Be specific.** Vague requests ("just let us know if you need anything") do not produce results. Come to coordination conversations with specific asks, specific timelines, and specific names.

**Confirm in writing.** A verbal agreement at a planning meeting may not survive a leadership change or a busy convention weekend. Follow up verbal agreements with a short written confirmation — an email is sufficient.

**Repeat key information at-con.** Pre-con coordination sets the foundation, but people forget. Confirm key contacts and procedures at the start of the convention with each relevant department.

---

### Safety

Safety is accessibility's most critical cross-department relationship. The two departments share responsibility for attendee welfare, with a clear division: Safety handles emergency response; accessibility handles accommodation and support. The line between the two is not always obvious in practice, and both teams need to understand where it falls.

#### What to Coordinate

**Escalation path:**
Establish clearly how accessibility escalates to Safety and what triggers escalation. Examples of situations that should involve Safety:
- A medical emergency or suspected overdose
- An attendee who is a danger to themselves or others
- A situation that requires physical intervention or removal

Situations that accessibility handles without Safety involvement:
- Attendee in the quiet room who is distressed but not in danger
- Accommodation requests or complaints
- Mobility aid assistance

The line is: Safety when there is a risk to physical safety; accessibility when there is a need for support or accommodation.

**At-con communication:**
Confirm radio channel or communication method for reaching Safety during the convention. Accessibility staff should have a direct way to reach Safety quickly in an emergency — not through a chain of intermediaries.

**Shared incidents:**
If an attendee interacts with both Safety and accessibility over the same incident, establish whether and how information is shared between departments. Attendee privacy applies; share only what is necessary for their welfare.

#### When to Coordinate
- Pre-con: establish escalation procedures and confirm communication method
- 30 days out: confirm at-con contacts and radio/communication assignments
- At-con: brief exchange at the start of the convention to confirm contacts are current

---

### IT

IT's primary role in accessibility operations is the quiet room camera system, but the relationship may extend to other technical needs depending on the convention.

#### What to Coordinate

**Quiet room camera:**
- Camera placement, installation, and access *(see Chapter 11: Quiet Room)*
- Who has access to the camera feed and how
- Process for accessibility to request a welfare check or flag a concern to IT

**Accessibility desk technical needs:**
- Power access at the desk location
- Any digital intake or logging systems the department plans to use
- Connectivity if the department uses apps or online tools

**At-con communication:**
Confirm how to reach IT during the convention for urgent quiet room issues.

#### When to Coordinate
- Venue assessment: identify quiet room candidate locations with IT input
- 3 months out: confirm camera placement plan and access procedures
- 30 days out: confirm at-con IT contact and communication method
- At-con setup: confirm camera is live and accessible before convention opens

---

### Registration

Registration controls the badge pickup process and the attendee registration system — both of which intersect directly with accessibility operations.

#### What to Coordinate

**Badge emblem application at pickup:**
If the convention will offer emblem application at badge pickup for pre-registered attendees, Registration needs to:
- Know what the emblem is and how to apply it
- Have a way to flag registered attendees with accessibility needs in their system
- Brief their staff on confidentiality — registration staff who apply emblems should know they are an accessibility marker and nothing more *(see Chapter 9)*

Confirm whether Registration is technically and operationally prepared to support this before committing to it in attendee-facing communications.

**Accessibility desk placement near registration:**
If the accessibility desk is near registration, coordinate on layout, traffic flow, and signage so attendees can easily find both.

**Pre-registration process:**
If attendees can pre-register accessibility needs before the convention, confirm with Registration how that information is captured, stored, and surfaced at badge pickup.

**Communication to attendees:**
Coordinate with Registration on how accessibility information is communicated in confirmation emails, the convention website, and at badge pickup. Attendees should know the quiet room exists, that the accessibility desk is available, and how to find both.

#### When to Coordinate
- 6 months out: confirm whether badge emblem application at pickup is feasible
- 3 months out: confirm emblem format, application process, and registration staff briefing plan
- 30 days out: confirm at-con Registration contact
- At-con: brief check-in at the start to confirm emblem supply and process is working

---

### Programming

Programming controls event schedules, room assignments, and major event logistics like parade routes. Many accessibility conflicts originate here — and most can be prevented with early coordination.

#### What to Coordinate

**Event layout review:**
Request the opportunity to review major event layouts — particularly the main events room, dealer's den, and artist alley — before they are finalized. Accessibility should assess:
- Accessible seating placement and quantity
- Mobility aid navigation through the space
- Sight lines and acoustic conditions for attendees with hearing or vision needs
- Interpreter placement if ASL coverage is planned *(see Chapter 6)*

**Parade and major event routes:**
Routes for fursuit parades and similar large-scale events should be reviewed for accessibility conflicts — particularly situations where the primary route bypasses elevator access or creates mobility barriers. These are far easier to adjust before the route is announced than after *(see Chapter 4: Venue Assessment)*.

**Accessible seating policy:**
Confirm with Programming what the policy is for accessible seating in programming spaces — how it is marked, how it is reserved, and how it is enforced. Accessibility and Programming should be aligned so staff can answer attendee questions consistently.

**Schedule awareness:**
Accessibility staff benefit from knowing the convention schedule — particularly high-attendance events that will generate desk volume, and events where attendees with specific needs (ASL interpretation, accessible seating) may need advance coordination.

#### When to Coordinate
- Venue assessment: flag any programming conflicts identified during walkthrough
- 6 months out: request to be included in event layout review process
- 3 months out: review parade route and major event plans; confirm accessible seating policy
- At-con: brief daily awareness of major programming to anticipate staffing needs

### Convention Leadership

The Accessibility Director reports to convention leadership and should maintain a relationship with the convention chair or board throughout the planning cycle. Key touchpoints:

- Budget request and approval *(see Chapter 5)*
- Staffing and volunteer coordination through convention systems
- Escalation for situations that exceed the department's authority — media inquiries, formal complaints, significant incidents
- Post-con recommendations and continuous improvement *(see Chapter 14)*

Leadership should be kept informed of significant issues during the convention, not surprised by them afterward.
